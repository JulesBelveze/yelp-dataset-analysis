He represented {{ushr|NJ|8}} from January 1997 until the redistricting of January 2013, and is a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]]. The district includes Pascrell's hometown of [[Paterson, New Jersey|Paterson]] as well as parts of Passaic, [[Bergen County, New Jersey|Bergen]] and [[Hudson County, New Jersey|Hudson]] Counties.\n\n==Early life, education, and academic career==\nThe grandson of [[Italian people|Italian]] immigrants, Pascrell was born in [[Paterson, New Jersey]], the son of Roffie J. (n\u00e9e Loffredo) and William James Pascrell. The original surname, Pascrelli, was changed at Ellis Island.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/pascrell.htm |title=Pascrell |publisher=Rootsweb.ancestry.com |date= |accessdate=2013-10-04}}</ref> He attended St. George\u2019s Elementary School, and in 1955, graduated from St. John the Baptist High School, where he was elected student council president. Pascrell went on to attend [[Fordham University]] in [[New York City]] and subsequently earned a [[Bachelor\u2019s degree|Bachelors]] in [[journalism]] and a [[Master\u2019s degree|Masters]] in [[philosophy]].\n\nPascrell spent 12 years as a high school teacher in [[Paramus, New Jersey]], during which he taught several courses including [[psychology]], before being hired as a professor at [[Fairleigh Dickinson University]]. Pascrell was appointed to the Paterson Board of Education, where he served as president of the Board. He also served on the Board of Trustees of [[Passaic County Community College]].\n\n==Early political career==\n\n===State Assembly===\nPascrell was elected to the [[New Jersey General Assembly]] in 1987, eventually rising to the position of Minority Leader Pro Tempore.<ref>[http://pascrell.house.gov/about/full-biography Full Biography], Bill Pascrell. Accessed November 20, 2016.</ref>  He won his seat in 1987 with 34% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=395029 |title=NJ General Assembly 35 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 3, 1987 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> He won re-election in 1989 (36%),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=214948 |title=NJ General Assembly 35 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 7, 1989 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> 1991 (29%),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=40321 |title=NJ General Assembly 35 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 5, 1991 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> 1993 (31%),<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=149569 |title=NJ General Assembly 35 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 2, 1993 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> and 1995 (33%).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=156653 |title=NJ General Assembly 35 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 7, 1995 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> He served alongside [[John Girgenti]] for his first two.terms and [[Alfred E. Steele]] for the remaining four.\n\n===Mayor of Paterson===\nIn May 1990, Pascrell was one of four candidates in Paterson's mayoral election with the winner to take the office that [[Frank Xavier Graves, Jr.|Frank Graves]], who had served since 1982, had vacated upon his death two months before the election. Pascrell won with 51% of the vote in the nonpartisan election<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=554609 |title=Paterson, NJ Mayor Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=May 8, 1990 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> and was re-elected in 1994 with 46% of the vote against his eventual successor [[Martin G. Barnes|Martin Barnes]], a Republican from the Third Ward, and former Mayor Thomas C. Rooney, Jr., a Democrat from the Sixth Ward.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=554608 |title=Paterson, NJ Mayor Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=May 10, 1994 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref>\n\nPascrell served as mayor of Paterson while he also served as an assemblyman. For years this was a common practice for New Jersey officeholders, but new laws have rescinded the practice. {{citation needed|date=November 2013}}\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\nIn 1996, Pascrell ran for [[New Jersey's 8th congressional district]] and defeated [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] incumbent [[William J. Martini|Bill Martini]] 51%-48%, a margin of 6,249 votes. Pascrell was likely helped by [[Bill Clinton]]'s easy victory in New Jersey in that year's presidential election.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=27187 |title=NJ District 8 Race |publisher=Our Campaigns |date=Nov 5, 1996 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> He has never faced another contest nearly that close; since then, he has been reelected with at least 62 percent of the vote.\n\n;2012\n{{Main article|United States House of Representatives elections in New Jersey, 2012}}\nAfter redistricting, Pascrell's home was placed in the newly redrawn [[New Jersey's 9th congressional district|9th district]]. Fellow Democratic congressman [[Steve Rothman]] decided to move into the reconfigured 9th and challenge Pascrell in the primary. Rothman's home in [[Fair Lawn, New Jersey|Fair Lawn]] had been drawn into a Republican-leaning district against Republican [[Scott Garrett]].<ref>{{cite web|author= |url=http://blog.nj.com/njv_editorial_page/2011/12/us_rep_steve_rothmans_challeng.html |title=U.S. Rep Steve Rothman's challenge to Bill Pascrell is bad for N.J., U.S. |publisher=The Star-Ledger |date=December 30, 2011 |accessdate=2012-06-09}}</ref> Geographically, the new district was more Rothman's district than Pascrell's.  Rothman had represented 53% of the new 9th, while Pascrell had represented 43% of the new 9th.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.dailyrecord.com/article/CN/20111228/NJNEWS/312280015/Dems-square-off-stay-Congress?odyssey=mod_sectionstories |title=Rothman to challenge Pascrell in 9th District Democratic battle |publisher=Daily Record |date= 2011-12-28 |accessdate=2013-09-10}}</ref>\n\nDespite this, Pascrell defeated Rothman in the Democratic primary on June 5, 31,435 to 19,947, capturing approximately 61.1% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web | title = Candidates for House of Representatives For PRIMARY ELECTION 06/05/2012 | work = Official election results | publisher = NJ Secretary of State | date = 2012-07-23 | url = http://www.njelections.org/2012-results/2012-official-primary-results-house-of-rep-0723.pdf | accessdate = 2013-09-10 }}</ref> In the general election, he defeated Rabbi Shmuley Boteach by 73.6% to 25.4%.<ref>[http://www.politico.com/2012-election/results/house/new-jersey/ 2012 House Races], [[Politico]]. He has a particular interest in fire safety, and authored the bill that created the Assistance to Firefighters Grant Program, which gives federal grants directly to all fire departments, including volunteer fire departments, which he calls \"the forgotten part of the public safety equation\".\n\nHe was also a member of the House Transportation Committee, where he worked to modernize roads, bridges, airports and mass transit systems. He has secured funding for reconstructing various dangerous roads and bridges throughout New Jersey, including bringing attention to the [[U.S. Route 46|Route 46]] corridor. In addition, he has helped craft legislation to renew federal surface transportation programs, providing funding toward New Jersey Transit. The new legislation tackled projects of rail expansion between Passaic and Bergen counties, bridge construction throughout Route 46, and the establishment of a bike-pedestrian path in [[South Orange, New Jersey|South Orange]].\n\nPascrell is an [[Italian American]] and has been outspoken about the representation of Italian-Americans in the media regarding what he sees as negative stereotypes in shows such as [[Home Box Office|HBO]]'s ''[[The Sopranos]]''. His Italian heritage was questioned by comedian [[Stephen Colbert]] of ''[[The Colbert Report]]'', who alleged in an interview that Pascrell could not truly be of Italian descent because Italian surnames must end with a vowel.
