A [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democrat]], he served as a member of the [[California State Senate]], representing the 34th Senate District.\n\nA graduate of [[Anaheim High School]], Correa earned a [[Bachelor of Arts]] in [[Economics]] from [[California State University, Fullerton|Cal State Fullerton]] as well as a [[Juris Doctor]] and [[Master of Business Administration]] from [[University of California, Los Angeles|UCLA]]. He was an investment banker, a real estate broker, and a college instructor.\n\n==Early career==\nCorrea's political career began in 1996 when he ran for the [[California State Assembly]] as the Democratic nominee in the 69th Assembly District. In a very close race, he lost to [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] incumbent [[Jim Morrissey (politician)|Jim Morrissey]] by just 93 votes.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://articles.latimes.com/1996-12-06/local/me-10398_1_recount-request|title=Vote Recount Called Off by Assembly Candidate|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|date=December 6, 1996|last=Warren|first=Peter}}</ref> In a 1998 rematch, Correa was elected to the Assembly when he defeated Morrissey 54% to 43%.<ref>{{cite news|title=Decision '98: The Final Count|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|date=November 5, 1998}}</ref>\n\nWhile a member of the Assembly, Correa served on several committees and was the chair of the Committee on Business and Professions, the Public Employees, Retirement and Social Security Committee; the Select Committee on Mobile Homes; and the Select Committee on [[Marine Corps Air Station El Toro|MCAS El Toro]] Reuse.\n\nCorrea was re-elected to the Assembly twice but was forced from office by California's [[term limit]]s law, which restricts members from serving more than three two-year terms.\n\nIn 2004, he campaigned for and was elected to the [[Orange County Board of Supervisors]], becoming the first Democrat to serve on the board since 1987.<ref>{{cite news|title=Orange County Elections|work=[[Los Angeles Times]]|date=November 3, 2004|last=Weikel|first=Dan}}</ref> He represented the First District, which includes the cities of Garden Grove, Santa Ana, and Westminster as well as unincorporated areas of the county including [[Midway City, California|Midway City]].\n\n==State Senate==\n[[File:Lou Correa.jpg|thumb|left|Correa during his time in the state Senate]]\nIn January 2006, Correa entered the race for the Democratic Party nomination for the California State Senate 34th District, a seat vacated by termed out Democratic State Senator [[Joe Dunn (California)|Joe Dunn]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.ocregister.com/news/-193239--.html|title=34th Senate race likely to get heated, expensive|work=[[The Orange County Register]]|date=January 26, 2006|last=Quach|first=Hanh Kim}}</ref>\n\nAfter defeating Assemblyman [[Tom Umberg]] in the contested June primary, Correa faced off against Republican Assemblywoman [[Lynn Daucher]] in the November general election. In yet another close race, Correa won the election with a margin of victory of 1,392 votes.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.ocregister.com/news/correa-188354-daucher-district.html|title=The Hot Senate Race|work=[[The Orange County Register]]|date=October 24, 2006|last=Wisckol|first=Martin}}</ref>  As of 2016, he is the last Democrat to serve on the Orange County Board of Supervisors.\n\nCorrea was sworn into the California State Senate on December 4, 2006.\n\nIn 2010, Correa was reelected against [[Anaheim, California|Anaheim]] City Councilwoman [[Lucille Kring]].\n\nIn 2014, he ran for the [[Orange County Board of Supervisors]] but was defeated.\n\nIn a January 27, 2015 special election, he again ran for the [[Orange County Board of Supervisors]], but was narrowly defeated by [[Garden Grove, California|Garden Grove]] [[city council|City Councilman]]  [[Andrew Do]].\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\nCorrea ran for the United States Congress for the 46th District in
