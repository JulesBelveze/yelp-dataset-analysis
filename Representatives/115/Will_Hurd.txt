Canseco ultimately won the general election but was unseated after one term in 2012.\n\n;2014\n{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Texas, 2014#District 23}}\nHurd once again ran for the 23rd district in the [[United States House of Representatives elections, 2014]]. After turning back the challenge of former U.S. Representative Quico Canseco, who had lost re-election in 2012 to Democrat [[Pete Gallego]] of [[Alpine, Texas|Alpine]] by 2,500 votes, Hurd unseated one-term incumbent Gallego.<ref name=\"NatlJWillHurdNov2014\"/> He was endorsed by the ''[[San Antonio Express-News]]''.<ref name=\"NatlJWillHurdNov2014\"/> Hurd conducted a post-election swing through some parts of his district that had heavily favored Gallego in the voting.<ref>[http://www.fortstocktonpioneer.com/community/article_a9bc110a-80a4-11e4-9c21-d7ec24b280dd.html U.S. rep-elect comes through town] ''The Fort Stockton Pioneer'' December 11, 2015</ref> He was also the only candidate ever to have been endorsed by CIA Director Robert Gates, who was a great admirer of Hurd's work for the CIA and disappointed by his departure to run for public office. Gates has said that Hurd \u201chas the character and the integrity and the leadership skills for higher office.\u201d<ref>Alberta, Tim; Will Hurd Is the Future of the GOP; Politico; May 15, 2017; [https://www.politico.com/magazine/story/2017/05/05/congressman-will-hurd-texas-republican-profile-215102]</ref>\n\n;2016\n{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Texas, 2016#District 23}}\nHurd was handily renominated for a second term in the Republican primary election held on March 1, 2016. He defeated William \"Hart\" Peterson, 39,762 votes (82.2 percent) to 8,590 (17.8 percent).<ref>{{cite web|url= \nhttps://enrpages.sos.state.tx.us/public/mar01_273_state.htm?x=0&y=3493&id=231|title=Republican primary returns|date=March 1, 2016|publisher=[[Texas Secretary of State]]|accessdate=March 2, 2016}}</ref> After winning renomination, Hurd began to distance himself from Republican presidential candidate [[Donald Trump]]. He opposed Trump's \"nasty rhetoric\" in reference to Muslims and Latinos and the candidate's proposal to build an $8 billion, [[Trump's border wall|1,000-mile long wall]] across the American border with Mexico. \"Building a wall is the most expensive, least-effective way to do border security,\" Hurd said in an interview. Hurd said he did not need [[Coattail effect|coattails]] from his party's presidential nominee: \"Anybody who is hoping on coattails or macro trends, is not doing his job.\"<ref>Bill Lambrecht, \"Hurd is staying out of Trump's shadow: Congressman distances self from potential GOP nominee\", ''San Antonio Express-News'', March 20, 2016, pp, 1, A24</ref>\n\nIn the rematch with Gallego, the Democrat again tried to tie Hurd to the Trump campaign, which was considered unpopular with Texas Hispanics. Again Hurd distanced himself from Trump: \"I never endorsed Donald Trump, and I cannot in good conscience support or vote for a man who degrades women, insults minorities, and has no clear path to keep our country safe. He should step aside for a true conservative to defeat [[Hillary Clinton]].\"<ref name=hurdgallegobattle>John W. Gonzalez, \"Hurd, Gallego battle grinds on\", ''San Antonio Express-News'', October 9, 2016, pp. 1, A18.</ref> Hurd claimed that Gallego as a representative had been insufficiently aggressive in support for veterans issues and was largely a tool of [[Nancy Pelosi]], the former [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|House Speaker]] from [[California]] known for her [[Liberal (politics)|liberal]] politics. A Gallego backer, Sheriff Joe Frank Martinez of Val Verde County, said that turnout would be the key to determine which of the two would represent the district in Congress.<ref name=hurdgallegobattle/> However, Hurd ran better in Val Verde County than many of those who opposed him had anticipated. Hurd drew 5,929 votes (45.3 percent) there to Gallego's 7,148 (54.7 percent). Hurd ran thirty-nine votes ahead of Trump in Val Verde County.<ref name=countytotals/>\t\n\nIn the general election, Hurd narrowly defeated Gallego, 110,577 (48.3 percent) to 107,526 (47 percent), with [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian]] Ruben Schmidt Corvalan of San Antonio earning the remaining 10,862 votes (4.7 percent).<ref>{{cite web|title=Nov. 8 general election results|url=https://elections.texastribune.org/2016/general-election-results/|publisher=The Texas Tribune|accessdate=16 November 2016}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist319_state.htm|title=Election Results|date=November 8, 2016|publisher=Texas Secretary of State|accessdate=December 17, 2016}}</ref> Hurd ran sufficiently well in the Bexar County portion of the district and in nearby [[Medina County, Texas|Medina]] and [[Uvalde County, Texas|Uvalde]] counties to offset Gallego's large margins in [[El Paso County, Texas|El Paso]] and [[Maverick County, Texas|Maverick]] counties, the latter of which encompasses the border city of [[Eagle Pass, Texas|Eagle Pass]].<ref name=countytotals>{{cite web|url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist319_race85.htm|title=U.S. Representative District 23 County Totals|publisher=Texas Secretary of State|accessdate=December 29, 2016}}</ref>\n\n;2018\nOn March 7, 2018, Hurd won the GOP primary with 80 percent of the vote.
