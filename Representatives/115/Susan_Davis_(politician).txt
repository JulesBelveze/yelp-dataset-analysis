She is a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]]. The district includes central and eastern portions of the city of [[San Diego, California|San Diego]], as well as eastern suburbs such as [[El Cajon, California|El Cajon]], [[La Mesa, California|La Mesa]], [[Spring Valley, San Diego County, California|Spring Valley]] and [[Lemon Grove, California|Lemon Grove]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://wedrawthelines.ca.gov/downloads/meeting_handouts_082011/map_20110815_ap_cd_53_certified.pdf|title=District 53 |work=California Redistricting Commission certified map|publisher=Healthy City|accessdate=5 January 2013}}</ref>\n\n==Early life, education and career==\n\nDavis was born Susan Carol Alpert in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts]], the daughter of Dorothy M. \"Dora\" (n\u00e9e Wexler) and Dr. George R. Alpert. All of her grandparents were [[Jews|Jewish]] immigrants from [[Russia]].<ref>http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/%7Ebattle/reps/davissusan.htm</ref> She has spent most of her life in California. She graduated from the [[University of California, Berkeley]] where she was a member of [[Delta Phi Epsilon (social)|Delta Phi Epsilon]] sorority.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dphie.org/distinguished.cfm |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2010-08-23 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20110719024607/http://www.dphie.org/distinguished.cfm |archivedate=2011-07-19 |df= }}</ref>  She earned a master's degree in [[social work]] from the [[University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill]]. Her husband Steve Davis was a doctor in the [[United States Air Force|Air Force]] during the Vietnam War. After returning to California, she became a social worker in San Diego.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.house.gov/susandavis/bio.shtml |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2015-11-08 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20091008105437/http://www.house.gov/susandavis/bio.shtml |archivedate=2009-10-08 |df= }}</ref>\n\n==Early political career==\n\nDavis became active in politics through her membership in the local branch of the [[League of Women Voters]], of which she became president in 1977. She was elected to the San Diego School Board in 1983. She served there until 1992, including two years as president of the body.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://sdjewishjournal.com/sdjj/tag/congresswoman-susan-davis/|title=Politics, accidentally|date=January 2, 2015|work=San Diego Jewish Journal|accessdate=26 March 2018}}</ref><ref name=\"text\">{{cite web|url=http://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=199920000HR68|title=Bill Text - HR-68|work=House Resolution, California Legislature|accessdate=26 March 2018}}</ref>\n\nIn 1994, she was elected to the [[California State Assembly]], and was reelected in 1996 and 1998. In the Assembly, Davis chaired the Committee on Consumer Protection, Government Efficiency and Economic Development. She authored a state law giving women direct access to their [[Obstetrics and gynaecology|OB/Gyn doctors]] without requiring a referral from their primary care physicians. Other legislation she authored established the right of a patient to obtain a second medical opinion and allowed frail senior citizens to remain in their homes while receiving state-funded nursing care. She introduced laws to reward high-achieving teachers and to establish after-school programs at public schools.<ref name = \"text\"/>\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n[[File:Susan Davis marching in the 2014 San Diego LGBT Pride Parade.jpg|thumbnail|left|Susan Davis marching in the 2014 San Diego LGBT Pride Parade]]\nIn 2000, Davis challenged three-term [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] incumbent [[Brian Bilbray]] in what was then the 49th District, winning with 50 percent of the vote. Her district was renumbered the 53rd District after the [[2000 United States Census|2000 Census]] redistricting and made somewhat more Democratic than its predecessor. She has been reelected eight times since then without much difficulty. She is the first [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democrat]] to represent what is now the 53rd district for more than one term in over half a century.  The only other Democrat to represent this district since the [[Harry Truman]] administration, [[Lynn Schenk]], was toppled by Bilbray in the [[Republican Revolution|1994 Republican wave]].\n\nDavis introduced a federal version of the California OB/Gyn law she authored at the start of every Congress from 2001 to 2009.  Provisions of her OB/Gyn bill were included in the [[Affordable Care Act|health care reform bill]] enacted into law.\n\nIn 2011, Davis voted for the [[National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2012]] as part of a controversial provision that allows the government and the military to indefinitely detain American citizens and others without trial.<ref>http://www.ibtimes.com/ndaa-bill-how-did-your-congress-member-vote-384362</ref>\n\nDavis is a member of the [[New Democrat Coalition]]<ref>{{cite web|title=Members|author=|url=https://newdemocratcoalition-himes.house.gov/members|format=|publisher=New Democrat Coalition|date=|accessdate=2 February 2018}}</ref> and portrays herself as working across party lines. She serves on the House Armed Services Committee. She also serves on the Education and Workforce Committee, where she is the Ranking Member of the Subcommittee on [[United States House Education Subcommittee on Higher Education and Workforce Training|Higher Education and Workforce Development]].\n\nIn 2012, Davis filed a lawsuit to recover over $150,000 in campaign funds from her former campaign treasurer, Kinde Durkee.<ref>{{cite book
