In 2002, he defeated Scott Armey, the son of [[House Majority Leader]] and then-U.S. Representative [[Dick Armey]], in a primary [[runoff election]]. Prior to his election, he practiced as a doctor of [[obstetrics and gynecology]].\n\nBurgess is a member of the congressional [[Tea Party Caucus]], and he has been involved in the debates over [[Health care reform in the United States|health care reform]] and [[Energy policy of the United States|energy policy]]. He opposes abortion, is unsure of the extent of the contribution of human activity to [[global warming]], supports President [[Donald Trump]]'s restrictions on travel from Muslim-majority countries and refugee immigration, and supports the repeal of the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act]].\n\n==Early life, education, and medical career==\nMichael Burgess was born in [[Rochester, Minnesota|Rochester]], [[Minnesota]], the son of Norma (n\u00e9e Crowhurst) and Harry Meredith Burgess; his paternal family immigrated from [[Nova Scotia]], a province of [[Canada]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/~battle/reps/burgess.htm|title=burgess|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref> He graduated from [[University of North Texas|North Texas State University]] (now the University of North Texas) in 1972 and graduated from the [[medical school]] at [[The University of Texas Health Science Center at Houston]] in 1977.<ref name=\"bio\">{{cite web |url=http://burgess.house.gov/about/ |accessdate=2012-06-15 |title=Congressman Michael C. Burgess M.D.}}</ref> He completed a residency in Obstetrics and Gynecology at [[Parkland Memorial Hospital]] in [[Dallas, Texas]].<ref name=\"Bio\">{{cite web |title=Rep. Michael Burgess (R\u2013Texas) |publisher=[[Congressional Quarterly]] |url=http://www.rollcall.com/members/14812.html |accessdate=2011-10-16}}</ref> Burgess is an [[Reformed Episcopal Church|Anglican]].<ref>{{cite speech |first=Michael |last=Burgess |title=One Minute Speech Given in Recognition of the Guest Chaplain |url=http://chaplain.house.gov/chaplaincy/display_gc.html?id=403 |location=[[Dallas, Texas]] |date=2006-07-26 |accessdate=2012-06-12}}</ref>\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\nBurgess, who had never held any public office and voted in the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] primaries in 1990, 1992, and 1994, entered in the 2002 Republican [[primary election]] to replace U.S. Congressman and House Majority Leader [[Dick Armey]]. His primary opponent was Armey's son, Scott. The district, comprising the majority of [[Denton County, Texas|Denton County]], was strongly Republican, and political pundits had predicted that whoever won the primary would not only win the general election, but [[tantamount to election|be assured of at least a decade in Congress]].<ref name=\"D Magazine Article\">{{cite journal |url=http://www.dmagazine.com/Home/2002/11/01/The_Rise_and_Fall_of_Scott_Armey.aspx |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120925030003/http://www.dmagazine.com/Home/2002/11/01/The_Rise_and_Fall_of_Scott_Armey.aspx |dead-url=yes |archive-date=2012-09-25 |title=The Rise and Fall of Scott Armey |last=Michalski |first=Dan |publisher=[[D Magazine]] |date=2002-11-01 |accessdate=2011-09-13}}</ref>  Using the campaign slogan \"My dad is NOT Dick Armey\", Burgess touted the support of medical [[Political Action Committee]]s and organizations like the [[National Beer Wholesalers Association]].<ref name=\"D Magazine Article\" /><ref name=\"2002 donations\">{{cite web |url=http://www.opensecrets.org/politicians/summary.php?cycle=2004&type=I&cid=N00025219&newMem=N |title=Michael Burgess Campaign Finance |publisher=Open Secrets |accessdate=2011-09-13}}</ref> Burgess took second place in the primary behind Armey, finishing with 23% of the vote to Armey's 45%. Since neither candidate earned the required majority of votes, the election led to a primary [[Two-round system|runoff election]]. Before the runoff, ''[[The Dallas Morning News]]'' released a series of articles alleging that Armey used his influence as a judge to procure county jobs and contracts for his friends. The report hurt Armey's campaign, and Burgess won the runoff with 55% of the vote. He won the general election with 75% of the vote.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=1289|title=Our Campaigns - TX District 26 Race - Nov 05, 2002|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref>\n\nBurgess has won re-election seven times:\n*66% in 2004<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=4377|title=Our Campaigns - TX - District 26 Race - Nov 02, 2004|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref>\n*60% in 2006<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=207730|title=Our Campaigns - TX - District 26 Race - Nov 07, 2006|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref>\n*60% in 2008<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=401692|title=Our Campaigns - TX - District 26 Race - Nov 04, 2008|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref>\n*67% in 2010<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=526140|title=Our Campaigns - TX - District 26 Race - Nov 02, 2010|publisher=|accessdate=29 September 2016}}</ref>\n*68% in 2012<ref>{{cite news| url=http://elections.huffingtonpost.com/2012/results/texas | work=Huffington Post | title=Election results}}</ref>\n*nearly 84% in 2014 (for the first time, Burgess did not draw a Democratic challenger, and ran against nominal Libertarian opposition)\n\nBurgess won his eighth term in the U.S. House in the general election held on November 8, 2016. With 211,730 (66.4 percent) votes, he defeated the Democrat Eric Mauck and the [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian]] Mark Boler, who polled 94,507\t(29.6 percent) and 12,843\t(4 percent), respectively.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist319_state.htm|title=Election Results|date=November 8, 2016|publisher=Texas Secretary of State|accessdate=December 17, 2016}}</ref>\n\n===Tenure===\n[[File:Kerry Burgess McCain Udall Lieberman 2011 crop.jpg|alt=A man in a dark striped suit, standing, is speaking between two other men while gesturing with his hands. Why is it so hard to think they feel pain?}}\n\nAlthough he never used the phrase specifically, Burgess' comments were widely regarded as referring to fetal [[masturbation]], and they attracted national attention and controversy. In May 2009, ''[[Congressional Quarterly]]'' noted that Burgess had \"become a prominent voice on health care issues\" in the U.S. House.<ref>{{cite journal |url=http://www.cqpolitics.com/wmspage.cfm?docID=news-000003111782&cpage=2 |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090514093126/http://www.cqpolitics.com/wmspage.cfm?docID=news-000003111782&cpage=2 |archivedate=2009-05-14 |title=Luntz Shapes GOP Messages on Health Care |last=Armstrong |first=Drew |publisher=[[Congressional Quarterly]] |date=2009-05-06 |accessdate=2009-08-21}}</ref> Since the [[111th United States Congress]], he has chaired the Congressional Health Care [[Congressional caucus|caucus]], of which he is the only official member.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://health.burgess.house.gov/About/ |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090429221253/http://health.burgess.house.gov/About/ |dead-url=yes |archive-date=2009-04-29 |title=Congressional Health Care Caucus \u2013 About |accessdate=2012-06-10 |year=2009}}</ref>\n\nBurgess supports the repeal of the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act]], also known as the ACA or Obamacare. At the 2017 [[Conservative Political Action Conference]], Burgess said he favored covering fewer Americans with [[health insurance]]. In 2011, he submitted an amendment to the 2012 Energy and Water Appropriations Act to defund part of the act that established higher efficiency standards for household light bulbs.<ref>{{cite news |newspaper=[[Houston Chronicle]] |date=2011-07-15 |last=French |first=Lauren |url=http://blog.chron.com/txpotomac/2011/07/michael-burgess-light-bulb-amendment-passes-house/ |accessdate=2012-09-24 |at=Texas on the Potomac |title=Michael Burgess' Light Bulb Amendment Passes House}}</ref> However, Burgess' claims that the standards represented a \"ban\" on conventional lightbulbs were rated as \"Mostly False\" by the [[Fact checker|fact checking]] website [[PolitiFact.com]].<ref>{{cite web |date=2011-06-15 |last=Selby |first=Gardner |title=Michael Burgess Says Stores Will be Barred from Selling 100-watt Bulbs in 2012 |editor-first=Jody |editor-last=Seaborn |url=http://www.politifact.com/texas/statements/2011/jul/28/michael-burgess/michael-burgess-says-stores-will-be-barred-selling/ |accessdate=2012-09-24}}</ref> On April 30, 2015, Burgess once again introduced an amendment to the $35.4 billion fiscal 2016 energy and water spending bill which would defund the [[United States Department of Energy|Department of Energy]] enforcement of incandescent light bulb efficiency standards, a measure which passed 232-189, largely on a party line vote <ref>Marcos, Christina [http://thehill.com/blogs/floor-action/house/240749-house-adopts-proposal-to-halt-incandescent-light-bulb-standards \"House adopts proposal to halt incandescent light bulb standards\"], ''The Hill'', May 01, 2015, Retrieved May 3, 2015</ref>\n\nBurgess is skeptical of the [[scientific opinion on climate change|scientific consensus on climate change]].
