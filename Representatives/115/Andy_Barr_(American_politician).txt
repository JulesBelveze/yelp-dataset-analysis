Donna R. (Faulconer) Barr.<ref name=mulls>Brammer, \"GOP's Barr mulls run for Congress\"</ref> The Barr family has been in Lexington for generations, and Barr Street in that city is reportedly named for one of Barr's ancestors.<ref name=fineline>Cheves, \"Barr says he offers respite from 'career politicians'\"</ref> His father founded the accounting firm of Barr, Anderson and Roberts and his mother is a deacon in the [[Episcopal Diocese of Lexington]].<ref name=mulls/>\n\nBarr graduated from [[Henry Clay High School]] in [[Lexington, Kentucky]], in 1992.<ref name=2009announce>Musgrave, \"Former Fletcher aide running for Congress\"</ref> He attended the [[University of Virginia]] where, as a contributor to a conservative campus publication called ''The Virginia Advocate'', he was highly critical of then-[[President of the United States|President]] [[Bill Clinton]] for allegedly [[Draft evasion|evading the draft]]. While in college, he was also an intern for [[United States Senate|U.S. Senator]] [[Mitch McConnell]] and the [[Republican National Committee]] as well as a member of [[Sigma Alpha Epsilon]]. In 1993, at age 19, he was arrested in [[Key West, Florida]], and charged with possessing a fake [[Mississippi]] [[driver's license]]. He pleaded guilty to the charge and was sentenced to eight hours of community service.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Brammer|first1=Jack|title=Spending by outside groups in Chandler-Barr race tops $1 million|url=http://www.kentucky.com/news/politics-government/article44381478.html|accessdate=8 May 2018|publisher=Lexington Herald Leader|date=October 9, 2012}}</ref>\n\nBarr graduated with a bachelor's degree in government and philosophy in 1996.<ref name=fineline/> From 1996 until 1998, he worked as a legislative assistant to [[Jim Talent]], then a [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] from [[Missouri]].<ref name=2009announce /> In 1999, he was charged with [[public intoxication]] in Lexington, a charge that was dismissed four months later.<ref name=fineline/> In 2001, Barr earned a law degree from the [[University of Kentucky College of Law]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Garland 'Andy' Barr|url=https://www.washingtontimes.com/elections/candidates/garland-andy-barr-60705/|publisher=Washington Times|accessdate=8 May 2018}}</ref>\n\n==Law career==\nCommencing practice in Lexington, he joined the Fayette County Bar Association Young Lawyers Section and co-founded the Lexington Charity Club&nbsp;\u2013 a non-profit organization of young men raising money for charitable causes&nbsp;\u2013 with Lee Greer and Rob Lewis.<ref name=patrick>Patrick, \"A Different Brown-Bag Meeting\"</ref> In 2002, he joined the liability defense service group and the business litigation service group at the Lexington law firm of [[Stites & Harbison]].<ref name=sah>\"Personnel File\". ''Lexington Herald-Leader''</ref> While there, he worked for former [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic]] [[Attorney General of Kentucky|Kentucky Attorney General]] and future [[Governor of Kentucky|Governor]] [[Steve Beshear]], who urged him to get involved in state politics.<ref name=2009announce /> Barr and colleague Brad Cowgill were employed by Republican gubernatorial candidate [[Ernie Fletcher]] in 2003 to fight charges that Fletcher's running mate, [[Hunter Bates]], did not meet the state's residency requirements for eligibility for the office of [[Lieutenant Governor of Kentucky|lieutenant governor]].<ref name=fineline /> A judge ruled against Bates, and he was dropped from the ticket.<ref name=fineline />\n\n===Fletcher administration===\n[[File:Ernie Fletcher.jpg|thumb|right|upright|alt=A man with dark hair and glasses wearing a black jacket and tie and white shirt|Ernie Fletcher employed Barr during his gubernatorial administration.]]\nAfter [[Kentucky gubernatorial election, 2003|Fletcher won the election]], Barr was named to the governor-elect's transition team in the Public Protection and Regulation Cabinet.<ref name=fletchertransition>\"Fletcher Transition Team Members\". ''The Kentucky Post''</ref> Ultimately, Fletcher chose Barr as general counsel for the governor's office of local development.<ref name=link>Alessi, \"State Office Stopped Recruiting Candidates\"</ref> When Fletcher declared April to be Child Abuse Prevention Month in Kentucky, Barr wrote Fletcher's speech for the occasion.<ref name=fineline /> While researching the speech, he made contact with the non-profit group Prevent Child Abuse in Kentucky.<ref name=fineline /> He became interested in the organization's mission and was elected to its board of directors in 2004; he served as vice-president of the organization in 2007 and president in 2008 and 2009.<ref name=fineline /><ref>Wingo, \"Personnel File\" (2004, 2007, June 2008, and 2009)</ref>\n\nFletcher's term in office was marred by a hiring scandal that involved violations of the state [[merit system]].<ref name=2009announce /> Barr was not implicated in the scandal; he told the ''[[Lexington Herald-Leader]]'' that Fletcher's Local Initiatives for a New Kentucky (LINK) outreach program&nbsp;\u2013 a sub-unit of the office of local development&nbsp;\u2013 stopped recruiting and vetting individuals for merit positions in the executive branch after he briefed officials about an opinion issued by the Executive Branch Ethics Commission during the administration of Fletcher's predecessor, [[Paul E. Patton]], regarding acceptable and unacceptable hiring under the merit system.<ref name=link /> The ''Herald-Leader'' later requested copies of any employment recommendations made by LINK employees, but Barr refused the request, citing an exemption in [[Freedom of information legislation (Kentucky)|Kentucky's Open Records Act]] that provides exemptions for \"preliminary drafts, notes and correspondence\" of state employees.<ref name=openrecords>Cheves, \"Fletcher Disbands Outreach Agency, Keeps Its Activities Secret\"</ref>\n\nIn 2007, Fletcher's general counsel resigned to become executive director of the Kentucky Bar Association; deputy general counsel David E. Fleenor was elevated to general counsel, and Barr replaced Fleenor as deputy general counsel.<ref name=dgc>\"N. Kentucky Police Chief is Charged with DUI\". ''Lexington Herald-Leader''</ref> In this capacity, he authored a defense of Fletcher's executive order that the [[Ten Commandments]] be posted in the rotunda of the [[Kentucky State Capitol|state capitol]] alongside other historical documents.<ref name=fineline /> Fletcher was [[Kentucky gubernatorial election, 2007|defeated for reelection in 2007]] and before his term expired, he named Barr to the state Public Advocacy Commission.<ref name=pac>Alessi, \"Fletcher rewards supporters Scores Appointed to State Boards, Commissions\"</ref>\n\n=== Post-Fletcher administration ===\nIn April 2008, Barr returned to private practice as an associate at the law firm of Kinkead and Stilz; he also worked as a part-time instructor, teaching constitutional law at the University of Kentucky.<ref name=\"2009announce\" /><ref name=\"pf2008\">Wingo, \"Personnel File\" (April 2008)</ref> He was chosen as an alternate delegate to the [[2008 Republican National Convention]] and served as vice-president of the Fayette County Republican Party.<ref name=\"2008altdelegate\">Alessi, \"GOP delegates upset two groups\"</ref><ref name=\"2008vp\">Brammer, \"Lineup of candidates for fall is taking shape\"</ref>\n\n==Congressional campaigns==\n\n===2010===\n[[File:Benchandler.jpeg|thumb|left|upright|alt=A man with brown hair and glasses wearing a black jacket, gray shirt, and red patterned tie|Ben Chandler, Barr's opponent in 2010 and 2012]]\nAfter forming an exploratory committee in September 2009, on November 10, 2009, Barr became the first Republican to formally announce that he would seek his party's nomination to challenge incumbent 5th district congressman Ben Chandler.<ref name=2009announce /> In the announcement, he touted his opposition to the [[Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act]] (which Chandler had also opposed) and the [[American Clean Energy and Security Act]] (which Chandler supported).<ref name=2009announce /> Barr's campaign raised far more money than those of any of his five opponents in the Republican [[primary election|primary]].<ref name=2009nominated>Cheves, \"Republicans pick Barr to challenge Chandler\"</ref> Barr garnered 31,255 votes in the primary, while his opponents' totals ranged from 4,789 to 1,880.<ref name=2009primaryresults>\"On the ballot\". ''Lexington Herald-Leader''</ref>\n\nIn an interview with [[WKYT-TV]] in July, Barr further elaborated on his platform.<ref name=wkyt>Cheves, \"Barr: Cut earmarks, federal spending\"</ref> Declaring his support for the free market, he denounced the recently signed [[Dodd\u2013Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act|Dodd\u2013Frank Act]] that enacted new regulations on the banking industry.<ref name=wkyt /> He called for an end to the practice of politicians [[Earmark (politics)|earmarking]] funds for special projects in their districts, a position that put him at odds with state party leaders like McConnell and long-time [[Kentucky's 5th congressional district|5th district]] Congressman [[Hal Rogers]].<ref name=wkyt /> Although he supported strengthening security along the [[Mexico\u2013United States border|U.S. border with Mexico]] to curb illegal immigration, he stated his belief that fellow Republican Senate candidate [[Rand Paul]]'s plan to deny citizenship to U.S.-born children of illegal immigrants was a violation of the [[Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution|Fourteenth Amendment]].<ref name=wkyt />\n\nThe [[National Republican Congressional Committee]] targeted Barr's race against Chandler as part of their strategy to gain control of the House of Representatives during the [[United States elections, 2010|2010 midterm elections]], and [[Ohio]] Congressman [[John Boehner]]&nbsp;\u2013 who stood to become [[Speaker of the United States House of Representatives|Speaker of the House]] if the Republicans gained a majority&nbsp;\u2013 visited the state to campaign on Barr's behalf.<ref name=chandlerwin>Cheves, \"Chandler declares win as GOP takes House\"</ref> Substantial amounts of money from political groups outside the state aided both candidates and spawned a number of negative campaign ads.<ref name=chandlerwin /> Chandler started the barrage in August with ads alluding to Barr's arrest in [[Florida]] for using a fake ID and seeking to tie him to the wrongdoing of the Fletcher administration.<ref name=chandlerwin /> Barr countered with ads criticizing Chandler's support of cap-and-trade legislation&nbsp;\u2013 an unpopular vote in coal-dependent Kentucky&nbsp;\u2013 and his vote in favor of the [[American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009]], which Barr characterized as a waste of taxpayer money that did little to stimulate an economic recovery.<ref name=chandlerwin />\n\nUnofficial election day results showed Chandler scoring a narrow victory over Barr, but the race was so close that Barr refused to concede.<ref name=concede>Musgrave, \"Barr concedes to Chandler\"</ref> When the official results were released, Chandler had received 119,812 votes to 119,163 for Barr.<ref name=concede /> Barr petitioned [[Secretary of State of Kentucky|Kentucky Secretary of State]] [[Trey Grayson]] for a recanvass of the voting machines in the district, but this yielded only one additional vote for Barr.<ref name=concede /> On November 12, ten days after the election, Barr announced that he would concede the election to Chandler rather than requesting a full recount.<ref name=concede />\n\n===2012===\nOn June 9, 2011, Barr announced that he would again challenge Chandler for his seat in the [[United States elections, 2012|2012 elections]].<ref name=2012announce>Brammer, \"Barr to run for Congress again\"</ref> The announcement was immediately hailed by [[Texas]] Congressman [[Pete Sessions]], chairman of the National Republican Campaign election campaign.<ref name=2012primary>Brammer, \"Barr wins easily, setting up rematch with Chandler\"</ref> When Chandler decided not to attend the [[2012 Democratic National Convention]], Barr name=2012rconvention />\n\nBoth candidates began their TV ad campaigns with more positive ads; Barr's wife appeared in his first ad, touting him as a \"family man\", while Chandler tried to combat The positive tone did not hold as the race tightened, however. Although not a competitive race, Barr was able to garner wide support and raise an incredible amount of cash compared to the low funds challenger Elisabeth Jensen took in.
