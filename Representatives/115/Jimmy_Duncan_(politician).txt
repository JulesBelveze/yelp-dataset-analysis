A lawyer, former judge, and former long serving member of the [[Army National Guard]], he is a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. The district is based in [[Knoxville]]. [[Lincoln Memorial University]]'s [[Duncan School of Law]] in Knoxville is named after Duncan.<ref>http://www.knoxnews.com/news/2012/jan/19/judge-denies-lmus-request-to-force-aba-removal/#comments</ref> Duncan announced he will be not be running for reelection in 2018.\n\n==Early life, education, and legal career==\nDuncan was born in [[Lebanon, Tennessee]]. His \"paternal grandparents were small-areage farmers in [[Scott County, Tennessee|Scott County]], which in 1861 left Tennessee, refusing to follow the Volunteer State into the [[Confederate States of America|Confederacy]], and declared itself 'the Free and Independent [[state of Scott]].'\"<ref name=Kauffman>[[Bill Kauffman|Kauffman, Bill]] (2005-09-12) [http://www.amconmag.com/article/2005/sep/12/00011/ Volunteer Statesman], ''[[The American Conservative]]''</ref> Duncan's parents were Lois (Swisher) and [[John Duncan Sr.]], who \"hitchhiked into [[Knoxville, Tennessee|Knoxville]] with five dollars in his pocket,' and after an education at the [[University of Tennessee]] was elected mayor of Knoxville and then congressman.\"<ref name=Kauffman/> The elder Duncan was also a co-owner of the [[Tennessee Smokies|Knoxville Smokies]] of the \"[[Southern League (baseball)|Sally League]],\" for which his son \"was a batboy, a ball shagger, scoreboard operator, and, as a freshman at the University of Tennessee, the Smokies' public-address announcer.\"<ref name=Kauffman/> Duncan also worked as a grocery bagger and salesman at [[Sears]] while working his way through school. Duncan supported [[Barry Goldwater]]'s [[United States presidential election, 1964|1964]] presidential campaign, and sent the first paycheck he earned as a bagboy at the local [[A&P]] to the Goldwater campaign.<ref name=Kauffman/>\n\nDuncan graduated from the [[University of Tennessee at Knoxville]] in 1969 with a [[Bachelor of Journalism]] degree and subsequently received a [[Juris Doctor]] degree from [[George Washington University Law School]] in [[Washington, D.C.]] in 1973 and was admitted to the [[bar (law)|bar]] that same year. He also served in the [[United States National Guard|Army National Guard]] from 1970 to 1987. He was an [[Lawyer|attorney]] in private practice until he became a state court [[judge]] in [[Knox County, Tennessee]], where he served from 1981 to 1988.\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\nHe was first elected to [[United States House of Representatives|Congress]] in 1988, in a [[special election]] to succeed his late father, [[John Duncan Sr.]], and elected to the seat for a full term in his own right the same day. He has been re-elected every two years since then from a district that has been held continuously by Republicans (or their antecedents) since 1859, and by a Duncan since his father was first elected in 1964.<ref name=Kauffman/> He has never faced a serious or well-funded challenge for reelection, and was reelected without major-party opposition in four consecutive elections (1994 through 2000).\n\n===Tenure===\n[[File:U.S. Senators Bob Corker, Richard Burr, Lamar Alexander, Congressman John Duncan among others at the Great Smoky Mountains National Park in 2009.jpg|thumb|300px|U.S. Senators [[Bob Corker]], [[Richard Burr]], [[Lamar Alexander]], and Congressman John Duncan (third from right) among others at the [[Great Smoky Mountains National Park]] in 2009]]\nDuncan voted against authorizing the 2003 [[2003 invasion of Iraq|War in Iraq]] based on opposition to what he believed to be an unnecessary foreign involvement. He also opposed and voted against a June 2006 House declaration in support of the war.<ref>[http://seattletimes.nwsource.com/html/nationworld/2003067352_vote17.html NWSource.com]</ref> He was one of the most [[conservatism|conservative]] Republicans to do so.<ref name=\"VoteSmart\">[http://www.vote-smart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=H3711103&type=category&category=Conservative Vote-smart.org] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070710025447/http://www.vote-smart.org/issue_rating_category.php?can_id=H3711103&type=category&category=Conservative |date=2007-07-10 }}</ref> Duncan later remarked that the [[Iraq War]] vote had been\n\n{{quote|a tough one for me. I have a very conservative Republican district. My Uncle Joe is one of the most respected judges in Tennessee: when I get in a really serious bind I go to him for advice. I had breakfast with him and my two closest friends and all three told me that I had to vote for the war. It's the only time in my life that I've ever gone against my Uncle Joe's advice. When I pushed that button to vote against the war back in 2002, I thought I might be ending my political career.<ref name=Kauffman/>}}\n\nDuncan was among only six Republicans to vote against funding for the [[Iraq War]] on May 24, 2007.<ref>{{cite news |first=John |last=Bresnahan |url=http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2007/05/25/politics/politico/thecrypt/main2852390.shtml |title=McNerney Takes Tough Vote On The War |work=CBS News |publisher=[[The Politico]] |date=2007-05-25}}</ref> Duncan voted, along with three other Republicans, to withdraw U.S. troops from Iraq by April 2008 on July 12, 2007.<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2007/roll624.xml</ref>\n\nOn March 10, 2010, Duncan again joined three other Republicans in voting for the removal of troops from Afghanistan.<ref>http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2010/roll098.xml</ref> Duncan and [[Ron Paul]] were the only members of Congress to vote for the removal of troops from Afghanistan and against all recent bailout and stimulus bills.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rlc.org/2009/03/26/17-courageous/ |title=17 courageous Congressmen voted against all bailouts &#124; Republican Liberty Caucus |publisher=Rlc.org |date=2009-03-26 |accessdate=2010-08-29 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20100813174538/http://www.rlc.org/2009/03/26/17-courageous/ |archivedate=2010-08-13 |df= }}</ref>\n\nHe has criticized [[neoconservatism]] and supports a [[non-interventionist]] foreign policy.<ref>https://duncan.house.gov/syria-intervention-mistake</ref>\n\nDuncan is a member of the [[Liberty Caucus]], a group of [[libertarianism|libertarian-minded]] congressional Republicans.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.thelibertycommittee.org/|title= The Liberty Committee|accessdate=2007-06-24}}</ref>  Other members include [[Walter B. Jones]] of [[North Carolina]], [[Roscoe Bartlett]] of [[Maryland]], [[Scott Garrett]] of [[New Jersey]], and [[Jeff Flake]] of [[Arizona]]. A former neighbor of his district, [[Zach Wamp]] of the 3rd district, also belonged to the group during his tenure in the House.<ref name=\"nytimesmagazine\">{{cite news |url = https://www.nytimes.com/2007/07/22/magazine/22Paul-t.html?_r=2&oref=slogin&oref=slogin |title = The Antiwar, Anti-Abortion, Anti-Drug-Enforcement-Administration, Anti-Medicare Candidacy of Dr. Ron Paul |first = Christopher |last = Caldwell |work = The New York Times Magazine |date = 2007-07-22 |accessdate = 2007-07-21}}</ref> \n\nDuncan voted against the [[Wall Street bailout]]. In a column he explained his vote stating he \"thought it would be better in the long run not to adopt the socialist approach.\"<ref>{{cite web|title=Duncan Column on the Financial Bailout |date=October 20, 2008 |first=Jimmy |last=Duncan |url=http://www.house.gov/list/press/tn02_duncan/octfinbailout.shtml |work=Official House Site |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20090529111544/http://www.house.gov/list/press/tn02_duncan/octfinbailout.shtml |archivedate=May 29, 2009 |df= }}</ref> The American Conservative Union gave Duncan a 96% score for his voting record in 2013, higher than any other federal Representative in Congress from Tennessee.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.conservative.org/legislative-ratings |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2014-05-01 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140502002242/http://www.conservative.org/legislative-ratings |archivedate=2014-05-02 |df= }}</ref>\n\nThe [[Family Research Council]] has rated Duncan as a 92% or above since 2002<ref name=\"VoteSmart\"/> and the [[National Rifle Association|NRA]] has rated him in equally positive terms.<ref name=\"VoteSmart\"/> In 2012, Duncan received the number one spot in the 435-member House in the National Taxpayers Union's (NTU) annual ranking of Congress, earning him the \"Taxpayer Hero\" award.\n\nDuncan is a frequent contributor to ''[[Chronicles (magazine)|Chronicles]]'' and ''[[The American Conservative]],'' both magazines associated with the [[paleoconservative]] movement.  He has also contributed to numerous trade publications and Capitol Hill newspapers. Duncan has also voiced public support for returning the gold standard.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.salon.com/2012/12/03/tennessee_goper_floats_return_to_the_gold_standard/ |title=Tennessee GOPer Floats Return to the Gold Standard |date=Dec 3, 2012 |work=Salon}}</ref>\n\nOn April 2016, Duncan endorsed [[Donald Trump]] for the Republican presidential nomination.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.politico.com/story/2016/04/rep-jimmy-duncan-endorses-donald-trump-222667 |title=Rep. Jimmy Duncan endorses Donald Trump |author=KRISTEN EAST |date=2016-04-30 |work=politico}}</ref>\n\nIn February 2017 Representative Duncan refused to hold any town halls in his Congressional District after the election of President [[Donald Trump]], calling some of his constituents extremists, kooks and radicals.<ref>http://www.knoxnews.com/story/news/politics/2017/02/06/us-rep-jimmy-duncan-rejects-town-hall-requests-citing-extremists-kooks/97525388/</ref>\n\nOn July 31, 2017, Duncan announced that he would not run for reelection in 2018.
