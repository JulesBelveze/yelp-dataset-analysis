June 6, 1952) is an American politician and businesswoman. A [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]], she serves as the [[United States House of Representatives|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|TN|7}}; Blackburn was first elected to the post in 2002. She is the Republican Deputy Whip and a former member of the [[Tennessee Senate]].\n\nIn October 2017, Blackburn announced her campaign for the [[United States Senate|United States Senate seat]] being vacated by Senator [[Bob Corker]] in [[United States Senate election in Tennessee, 2018|2018]]. The Tennessee Republican Party (TNRP) removed seven (7) U.S. Senate Candidates from the ballot effectively eliminating the voter's choice for these opponents to Blackburn in the Republican Primary<ref>https://www.tennessean.com/story/news/2018/04/07/tennessee-election-2018-state-republicans-senate-governor-candidates/496294002/</ref> A Republican primary without these 7 opponents to Blackburn was nonetheless placed on the ballot in August 2018.  Blackburn is running as a Republican and is expected to face Democratic nominee [[Phil Bredesen]] in the November 2018 general election.\n\n==Early life and education==\nBlackburn was born Marsha Wedgeworth in [[Laurel, Mississippi]], the daughter of Mary Jo (Morgan) and Hilman Wedgeworth, who worked in sales and management.<ref>https://brentwoodhomepage.com/hilman-wedgeworth-wwii-veteran-father-of-rep-blackburn/</ref> She attended [[Mississippi State University]], earning a [[Bachelor of Science|B.S.]] in [[home economics]] in 1974.<ref>https://archive.org/stream/reveille691974miss#page/342/mode/2up</ref><ref name=MSU/><ref name=\"library.msstate.edu\">{{cite web|url=http://library.msstate.edu/cprc/blackburn.asp|title=Mississippi State University Libraries: Congressional and Political Research Center: Collections: The Marsha Blackburn Collection|website=Library.msstate.edu|accessdate=2017-02-26}}</ref> In college, she joined [[Chi Omega]]<ref name=CM/><ref name=Baker>Baker, Jackson, [http://www.memphismagazine.com/July-2011/Marsha-Blackburn/ ''Marsha Blackburn \u2013 Beacon of the Right''], ''Memphis Magazine'', July 2011; retrieved December 6, 2013.</ref> and worked as a student manager for the [[Southwestern Company]], selling books door-to-door.<ref name=MSU>[http://library.msstate.edu/cprc/blackburn.asp The Marsha Blackburn Collection web page], Mississippi State University Congressional and Political Research Center; retrieved December 5, 2013.</ref> She is a former beauty-pageant winner.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thehill.com/capital-living/24096-understanding-the-beauty-queen-politician|title=Understanding the beauty-queen politician|first=Ashley|last=Perks|date=15 September 2008|publisher=}}</ref>\n\n==Early political career==\nBlackburn's professional career began in 1973 when she was hired as a sales manager for Times Mirror, Inc. In 1975 she was named Director of Retail Fashion and Special Events of the [[Castner Knott]] Division of Mercantile Stores, Inc. She held this position until 1978, when she became the owner of Marketing Strategies, a promotion-event management and image consulting firm. She continues to run this business.<ref name=\"library.msstate.edu\"/>\n\nBlackburn was a founding member of the [[Williamson County, Tennessee|Williamson County]] [[Young Republicans]].<ref name=Baker/> She became chair of the Williamson County Republican Party in 1989 and served until 1991.<ref name=Baker/><ref>http://media.cq.com/members/6434</ref> In 1992, she was a candidate for [[United States Congress|Congress]] and a delegate to the [[1992 Republican National Convention]]. She lost the congressional race,<ref name=Baker/> but remained active in social and political venues.<ref name=MSU/>\n\nIn 1995, Blackburn was appointed executive director of the Tennessee Film, Entertainment, and Music Commission by Tennessee governor [[Don Sundquist]],<ref>https://www.newspapers.com/newspage/112821722/</ref><ref name=Baker/> and held that post through 1997.<ref>https://www.nashvillescene.com/arts-culture/article/13001516/tennessee-gets-a-new-film-commissioner</ref>  In 1998, she was elected to the [[Tennessee Senate]], where she served for six years and rose to be minority [[Whip (politics)|whip]].<ref name=MSU/>\n\nIn 2000, she took part in the effort to prevent the passage of a [[state income tax]] championed by Sundquist.<ref name=Baker/>\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n===Tenure===\n\n[[File:Marsha blackburn.jpg|thumb|Rep. 2008}}</ref>\n\n===Political campaigns===\nRedistricting after the [[2000 United States Census|2000 Census]] moved Blackburn's home from the 6th district into the 7th district. The 6th District's [[Democratic Party (US)|Democratic]] [[incumbent]] congressman, [[Bart Gordon]], had faced three tough races in the 1990s, including a near-defeat in 1994, in part due to the growing Republican trend in Nashville's suburbs. This was especially pronounced in Williamson County, the richest county in the state and the most Republican county in Middle Tennessee. It appeared that the Democratic-controlled Tennessee General Assembly wanted to protect Gordon by moving Williamson County into the already heavily Republican 7th District.<ref>{{cite news|title=2011 Redistricting TN|date=2010-01-12|work=TN Precinct Project|url=http://www.tnprecinctproject.com/2010/01/12/2011-redistricting-tn|first=Kent|last=Davis|accessdate=June 3, 2017}}</ref> To maintain approximately equal district sizes (as required by [[Wesberry v. Sanders]]) and to compensate for the substantial increase in the 7th's population by the addition of Williamson County, the legislature shifted some of the more Democratic parts of [[Clarksville, Tennessee|Clarksville]] to the nearby 8th district. This created a district that, in the words of ''Memphis Magazine'', stretched \"in reptilian fashion\" for 200 miles from eastern Memphis to southwest Nashville.<ref name=\"Baker\" />\n\nIn 2002, 7th District incumbent Republican congressman [[Ed Bryant]] decided to run for the [[United States Senate|Senate]] seat being vacated by [[Fred Thompson]]. Blackburn entered the primary to replace Bryant\u2014the real contest in this Republican stronghold. Of the four main candidates, she was the only one from the Nashville suburbs. The other three, future state senate majority leader [[Mark Norris (American politician)|Mark Norris]], conservative activist and future U.S. Attorney and Representative [[David Kustoff]], and city councilman Brent Taylor, were all from Memphis and its suburbs. She garnered the endorsement of the conservative [[Club for Growth]].<ref>Bianca Phillips, [http://www.memphisflyer.com/JacksonBaker/archives/2002/08/01/final-report-on-tennessee-elections Final Report on Tennessee Elections], ''Memphis Flyer'', August 1, 2002; retrieved March 7, 2016.</ref> The three Memphians split the vote in that area, allowing her to win the primary by nearly 20 percentage points.<ref>[http://www.dcpoliticalreport.com/members/2002/TNResults.htm 2002 Tennessee Congressional and Statewide Primary Results], ''D.C.'s Political Report''; retrieved March 7, 2016.</ref>\n\nIn the general election, she defeated Democratic nominee Tim Barron, with 70% of the vote. She was the fourth woman elected to Congress from Tennessee, but the first not to serve as a stand-in for her husband.<ref>[http://www.williamsonherald.com/news/local_news/article_f83e9e08-e226-11e5-a53c-e391dd021950.html \"Marsha Blackburn Named 2016 'Woman of the Year'\"], ''Williamson Herald'', March 4, 2016.</ref>\n\nShe ran unopposed for reelection in 2004, which is somewhat unusual for a freshman member of Congress, even from a district as heavily Republican as the 7th. A 2004 survey of congressional aides by the ''[[Washingtonian (magazine)|Washingtonian]]'' identified her as one of the three \"best newcomers\" in the House of Representatives.<ref>\"[http://www.washingtonian.com/2004/09/01/best-worst-of-congress/ Best and Worst of Congress]\", Washingtonian.com, September 1, 2004; retrieved March 7, 2016.</ref>\n\nRedistricting after the 2010 census made the 7th district more compact; it lost its shares of Nashville and Memphis while regaining all of Clarksville. However, it is no less Republican than its predecessor; with a [[Cook Partisan Voting Index]] of R+18, it is one of the most Republican districts in the South.<ref>\"[http://cookpolitical.com/file/2013-04-49.pdf Partisan Voting Index: Districts of the 113th Congress], ''The Cook Political Report''; accessed June 3, 2017.</ref>\n[[File:Marsha Blackburn Official.jpg|thumb|Rep.
