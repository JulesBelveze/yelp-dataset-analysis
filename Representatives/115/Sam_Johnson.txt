(4)<br>[[Prisoner of War Medal]]\n}}\n'''Samuel Robert Johnson''' (born October 11, 1930) is the [[United States Representative|U.S. Representative]] for {{ushr|TX|3}} serving in Congress since 1991. He is a member of the [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican Party]]. The district includes much of [[Collin County, Texas|Collin County]], an affluent suburban county north of [[Dallas]]. In October\u2013November 2015, he was the acting Chairman of the [[House Committee on Ways and Means]], where he also serves as chairman of the [[United States House Ways and Means Subcommittee on Social Security|Social Security Subcommittee]].\n\nJohnson is also a retired [[United States Air Force]] [[Colonel (United States)|Colonel]] and was a decorated fighter pilot in both the Korean War and the [[Vietnam War]] where in the latter he was an American [[prisoner of war]] in [[North Vietnam]] for nearly seven years.\n\nOn January 6, 2017, Johnson announced he will not run for reelection in 2018.<ref name=Politicoretire>{{cite news|url=http://www.politico.com/story/2017/01/sam-johnson-wont-run-for-reelection-233283|title=Texas Rep. Sam Johnson announces he won't run for reelection|author1=Scott Bland|author2=Kyle Cheney|publisher=[[Politico (magazine)|Politico]]|date=2017-01-06}}</ref> He is the oldest current member of the U.S. House of Representatives.\n\n== Early life and education ==\nJohnson grew up in Dallas and graduated from [[Woodrow Wilson High School (Dallas)|Woodrow Wilson High School]].<ref name=\"congbio\">U.S. Congress.[http://bioguide.congress.gov/scripts/biodisplay.pl?index=j000174 Biographical Directory of the United States Congress: Sam Johnson]</ref>  Johnson graduated from [[Southern Methodist University]] in his hometown in 1951, with a degree in business administration.  While at SMU, Johnson joined the [[Delta Chi]] social fraternity as well as the [[Alpha Kappa Psi]] business fraternity.<ref>{{cite book|title=Rotunda Yearbook|year=1951|publisher=Southern Methodist University|location=Dallas, Texas|page=284}}</ref> He attained a master's degree from the [[Elliott School of International Affairs]] of the [[George Washington University]] in 1976.\n\n== Military career ==\n[[File:Sam Johnson in flight school.jpg|thumb|left|Johnson in flight school in 1951]]\n[[File:Sam Johnson as first lieutenant.jpg|thumb|left|Johnson as a [[First Lieutenant]] in 1957]]\nJohnson served a 29-year career in the [[United States Air Force]], where he served as director of the [[United States Air Force Weapons School|Air Force Fighter Weapons School]] and flew the [[F-100 Super Sabre]] with the [[Thunderbirds (squadron)|Air Force Thunderbirds]] precision flying demonstration team. He commanded the [[31st Fighter Wing|31st Tactical Fighter Wing]] at [[Homestead AFB]], Florida and an air division at [[Holloman AFB]], New Mexico, retiring as a [[Colonel (United States)|colonel]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.samjohnson.house.gov/Biography/ |title=U.S. Congressman Sam Johnson : Serving the 3rd District of Texas |publisher=Samjohnson.house.gov |date= |accessdate=2010-08-29}}</ref>\n\nHe is a combat veteran of both the [[Korean War|Korean]] and [[Vietnam War]]s as a [[fighter pilot]]. During the Korean War, he flew 62 combat missions in the [[F-86 Sabre]] and shot down one [[Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-15]]. During the Vietnam War, Johnson flew the [[F-4 Phantom II]].\n\n;POW\nOn April 16, 1966, while flying his 25th combat mission in [[Vietnam]], he was shot down over [[North Vietnam]] and suffered a broken arm and back. He was a [[prisoner of war]] for nearly seven years, including 42 months in solitary confinement. During this period, he was repeatedly [[torture]]d.\n\nJohnson was part of a group of eleven U.S. military prisoners known as the [[Alcatraz Gang]], a group of prisoners separated from other captives for their resistance to their captors. They were held in \"Alcatraz\", a special facility about one mile away from the [[H\u1ecfa L\u00f2 Prison]], notably nicknamed the \"Hanoi Hilton\". He was elected to the [[Texas House of Representatives]] in 1984 and was re-elected four times.{{citation needed|date=March 2017}}\n\n===U.S. House of Representatives===\n[[File:Dick Cheney and Sam Johnson.jpg|thumb|right|Johnson and [[Dick Cheney]] in 2001]]\n[[File:Sam Johnson and Grover Norquist.jpg|thumb|right|Johnson and [[Grover Norquist]] in 2002]]\n[[File:Col. Oliver North and Congressman Sam Johnson.png|thumb|right|Johnson greeting [[Oliver North]] in 2006]]\n[[File:John McCain and Sam Johnson.jpg|thumb|right|Johnson with fellow [[Prisoner of war|POW]] [[John McCain]] in 2016]]\nOn May 8, 1991, he was  [[Texas's 3rd congressional district special election, 1991|elected to the U.S. House in a special election]] brought about by eight-year incumbent [[Steve Bartlett]]'s resignation to become [[List of mayors of Dallas|mayor of Dallas]]. Johnson defeated fellow [[Conservative (politics)|conservative]] Republican [[Thomas Pauken]], also of Dallas, 24,004 (52.6 percent) to 21,647 (47.4 percent).<ref>''Congressional Quarterly's Guide to U.S. Elections'' 6th ed., [[Washington, D.C.]], 2010, p. 1341</ref> Johnson thereafter won a full term in 1992 and has been reelected 12 times.  The 3rd has been in Republican hands since 1968. The Democrats did not even field a candidate in 1992, 1994, 1998, or 2004.\n\n====Elections====\n;2004\n{{see also|United States House of Representatives elections in Texas, 2004#District 3|United States House of Representatives elections in Texas, 2006#District 3|United States House of Representatives elections in Texas, 2008#District 3}}\nJohnson ran unopposed by the [[United States Democratic Party|Democratic Party]] in his district in the 2004 election. Paul Jenkins, an independent, and James Vessels, a member of the [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian Party]] ran against Johnson. Johnson won overwhelmingly in a highly Republican district. Johnson garnered 86% of the vote (178,099), while Jenkins earned 8% (16,850) and Vessels 6% (13,204).\n\n;2006\n\nJohnson ran for re-election in 2006, defeating his opponent Robert Edward Johnson in the Republican [[Primary election|primary]], 85 to 15 percent.<ref name=\"state1\">{{cite web |url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist.exe |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2014-02-22 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140109062336/http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist.exe |archivedate=2014-01-09 |df= }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dallasnews.com/sharedcontent/dws/news/politics/local/stories/DN-usreprndp_08met.ART.State.Edition3.aae3.html |title=News for Dallas, Texas &#124; Dallas Morning News &#124; Dallas-Fort Worth Politics &#124; The Dallas Morning News |publisher=Dallasnews.com |date=2006-03-08 |accessdate=2010-08-29}}</ref>\n\nIn the general election, Johnson faced Democrat [[Dan Dodd]] and Libertarian Christopher J. Claytor. Both Dodd and Claytor are [[United States Military Academy|West Point]] graduates. Dodd served two tours of duty in Vietnam<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.dandodd.us/Biography.html |title=Archived copy |accessdate=2006-05-04 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20060621210722/http://www.dandodd.us/Biography.html |archivedate=2006-06-21 |df= }}</ref> and Claytor served in [[Operation Southern Watch]] in Kuwait in 1992. [http://www.claytorforcongress.org]  It was only the fourth time that Johnson had faced Democratic opposition.\n\nJohnson retained his seat, taking 62.5% of the vote, while Democrat Dodd received 34.9% and Libertarian Claytor received 2.6%.  However, this was far less than in years past, when Johnson won by margins of 80 percent or more.\n\n;2008\n\nJohnson retained his seat in the House of Representatives by defeating the Democrat Tom Daley and Libertarian nominee Christopher J. Claytor in the 2008 general election. He won with 60 percent of the vote, an unusually low total for such a heavily Republican district.<ref name=\"state1\"/>\n\n;2010\n\nJohnson won re-election with 66.3 percent of the vote against Democrat John Lingenfelder (31.3 percent) and [[Libertarian Party (United States)|Libertarian]] Christopher Claytor (2.4 percent).<ref>{{cite web|url=http://elections.nytimes.com/2010/results/texas|title=Texas Election Results 2010|publisher=''[[The New York Times]]''|year=2010|accessdate=2011-03-15}}</ref>\n\n;2014\n\nJohnson handily won re-nomination to his 12th full term, twelfth full term, in the U.S. House in the Republican primary held on March 4. He polled 30,943 votes (80.5 percent); two challengers, Josh Loveless and Harry Pierce, held the remaining combined 19.5 percent of the votes cast.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://team1.sos.state.tx.us/enr/results/mar04_169_state.htm?x=0&y=218&id=176|title=Republican primary election returns, March 4, 2014|publisher=Texas Secretary of State|accessdate=March 6, 2014|deadurl=yes|archiveurl=https://archive.is/20140305180619/https://team1.sos.state.tx.us/enr/results/mar04_169_state.htm?x=0&y=218&id=176|archivedate=March 5, 2014|df=}}</ref>\n\n;2016\n\nJohnson won reelection to his 13th full term in the general election held on November 8, 2016. With 193,684 votes (61.2 percent), he defeated the Democrat Adam P. Bell, who polled 109,420 (34.6 percent). Scott Jameson and Paul Blair, the nominees of the Libertarian and [[Green Party (United States)|Green]] parties, polled 10,448 votes (3.3 percent) and 2,915 (0.92 percent), respectively.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist319_state.htm|title=Election Results|date=November 8, 2016|publisher=Texas Secretary of State|accessdate=December 17, 2016}}</ref>\n\n;2018\n\nThree days after being sworn in for his 14th term overall and his 13th full term, Johnson announced he would not run for reelection.<ref name=Politicoretire/>\n\n===Tenure===\n{{BLP sources|date=March 2013}}\nIn the House, Johnson is an ardent [[Conservatism (United States)|conservative]]. By some views, Johnson had the most conservative record in the House for three consecutive years, opposing [[pork barrel]] projects of all kinds, voting for more [[Individual Retirement Account|IRAs]] and against extending unemployment benefits. The conservative watchdog group [[Citizens Against Government Waste]] has consistently rated him as being friendly to taxpayers. Johnson is a signer of [[Americans for Tax Reform#Taxpayer Protection Pledge|Americans for Tax Reform]]\u2019s [[Taxpayer Protection Pledge]].<ref name =\"ATR\">{{cite web|title=The Taxpayer Protection Pledge Signers 112th Congressional List|url=http://s3.amazonaws.com/atrfiles/files/files/091411-federalpledgesigners.pdf|publisher=Americans for Tax Reform|accessdate=November 30, 2011}}</ref>\n\nJohnson is a member of the conservative [[Republican Study Committee]],<ref>{{cite web|title=Member List|author=|url=https://rsc-walker.house.gov/|format=|publisher=Republican Study Committee|date=|accessdate=21 December 2017}}</ref> and joined [[Dan Burton]], [[Ernest Istook]] and [[John Doolittle]] in refounding it in 1994 after [[Newt Gingrich]] pulled its funding.  He alternated as chairman with the other three co-founders from 1994 to 1999, and served as sole chairman from 2000-01.{{citation needed|date=March 2017}}\n\nOn the Ways and Means Committee, he was an early advocate and, then, sponsor of the successful repeal in 2000 of the earnings limit for [[Social Security (United States)|Social Security]] recipients. He proposed the Good Samaritan Tax Act to allow corporations to take a tax deduction for charitable giving of food. He chairs the [https://web.archive.org/web/20050624182242/http://edworkforce.house.gov/members/109th/mem-eer.htm Subcommittee on Employer-Employee Relations], where he has encouraged small business owners to expand their [https://web.archive.org/web/20050624173626/http://edworkforce.house.gov/press/press109/first/06jun/pensionintro060905.htm pension] and<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/bdquery/z?d109:h.r.00525:health |title=Bill Summary & Status \u2013 109th Congress (2005\u20132006) \u2013 H.R.525 \u2013 THOMAS (Library of Congress) |publisher=Thomas.loc.gov |date=2005-07-27 |accessdate=2010-08-29}}</ref> benefits for employees. In December 2016, Johnson introduced [https://www.congress.gov/bill/114th-congress/house-bill/6489?r=37 H.R. 6489], a bill that would decrease [[Social Security (United States)|Social Security]] [[Social Security (United States)#Benefits|payments to retired individuals]] and [[Social Security (United States)#Normal retirement age|require individuals to wait two additional years]] in order to qualify for full retirement payments.<ref>Wolff-Mann, Ethan. \"[https://finance.yahoo.com/news/gop-introduces-plan-to-massively-cut-social-security-222200857.html GOP introduces plan to massively cut Social Security]\". ''Yahoo Finance''. December 8, 2016; retrieved December 12, 2016.</ref>\n\nJohnson opposes calls for government intervention in the name of energy reform if such reform would hamper the market and or place undue burdens on individuals seeking to earn decent wages.
