The district includes parts of [[Philadelphia]] and [[Montgomery County, Pennsylvania|Montgomery County]]. He was previously a member of the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives]], representing the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives, District 170|170th District]] from 2009 to 2015.<ref name=\"boyle/\">{{cite web |url=http://www.pahouse.com/boyle/ | title=Rep. Brendan Boyle | publisher=PA House of Representatives | work=PA House of Representatives Official Website| accessdate=29 January 2015 |quote=Rep. Brendan Boyle resigned his PA House District 170 seat to serve as a member of the U.S. Congress. }}</ref>\n\n==Early life and education==\n\nBoyle is the elder of two sons. His father, Francis, is an Irish immigrant who came to the United States in 1970 from [[Glencolmcille]], [[County Donegal]], and works as a janitor for the [[SEPTA|Southeastern Pennsylvania Transportation Authority (SEPTA)]]. His late mother, Eileen, was the child of Irish immigrants from [[County Sligo]]; she worked as a [[School District of Philadelphia|Philadelphia School District]] crossing guard for over 20 years.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Brendan Boyle, son of Donegal emigrant,  wins seat in Congress |url=http://www.irishtimes.com/news/world/us/brendan-boyle-son-of-donegal-emigrant-wins-seat-in-congress-1.1989833|website = The Irish Times|accessdate = 2015-12-05}}</ref>\n\nBoyle was born and raised in the [[Olney, Philadelphia|Olney]] neighborhood of [[Philadelphia, Pennsylvania]]. He attended [[Cardinal Dougherty High School]] before receiving an academic scholarship to the [[University of Notre Dame]], where he graduated with a degree in Government in 1999, completing the Hesburgh Program in Public Service. After working for several years as a consultant with the U.S. Department of Defense, including Naval Sea Systems Command, he attended graduate school at [[Harvard University]]'s [[John F. Kennedy School of Government]], where he earned a master's degree in Public Policy.<ref name=\"biodata\">[http://voteboyle.com/about-brendan Brendan Boyle biodata], voteboyle.com; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref>\n\n==Pennsylvania House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\nBoyle ran unsuccessfully for the Pennsylvania House of Representatives in 2004 and 2006, losing both times to longtime Republican incumbent [[George T. Kenney]]. On November 4, 2008, Boyle defeated Republican Matthew Taubenberger, son of [[Philadelphia mayoral election, 2007|2007 mayoral candidate]] [[Al Taubenberger]], by a margin of 15,442 (59.2%) to 10,632 (40.8%) to win the election to succeed Kenney,<ref name=\"pahouseelectionreturns\">[http://www.electionreturns.state.pa.us/ElectionsInformation.aspx?FunctionID=13&ElectionID=28&OfficeID=13#161 Pennsylvania election returns] (2008); accessed November 9, 2014.</ref> becoming the first Democrat ever elected to represent the [[Pennsylvania House of Representatives, District 170|170th district]].<ref name=\"biodata\"/><ref name=\"pahouseelectionreturns\"/><ref name=\"Shaheeli\"/>\n\nOn November 2, 2010, Boyle won re-election, defeating Republican Marc Collazzo by a margin of 64% to 36%.<ref name=\"pahouseelectionreturns\"/><ref>[http://www.electionreturns.state.pa.us/ElectionsInformation.aspx?FunctionID=13&ElectionID=39&OfficeID=13#170 Pennsylvania election returns (2010]; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref>\n\nIn the 2012 election cycle, Boyle ran unopposed and was selected as Chairman of the Pennsylvania House Democratic Campaign Committee, the campaign arm of the Pennsylvania House Democratic Caucus.<ref name=\"hdcc\">{{cite web|author=Keegan Gibson|title=Exclusive: Boyle to Chair HDCC|publisher=PoliticsPA|date=June 21, 2011|url=http://www.politicspa.com/exclusive-boyle-chair-hdcc/25511|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>\n\nBoyle ran unopposed in the 2014 election cycle and resigned his seat on January 2, 2015 prior to being sworn in as a member of the [[United States House of Representatives]]. He was succeeded by [[Martina White]].\n\n===Tenure===\nAs a state lawmaker, Boyle's focus was on greater educational access, healthcare and greater economic equality.\n\nAs the first member of his family to attend college, he prioritized greater access to higher education. During his first term in office, he introduced the REACH Scholarship program, which would offer tuition-free public college for qualifying Pennsylvania students. [[File:Brendan-boyle.jpg|left|thumb|179x179px|Then state Rep. Boyle speaking at a press conference in the Pennsylvania State Capitol, June 2013.]]He fought cuts to public K-12 and higher education funding, and supported greater investment in infrastructure, voting in 2013 for legislation (passed into law as Act 89) that provided the first comprehensive transportation funding overhaul in Pennsylvania in nearly 20 years, providing several billion dollars in new funds for roads, bridges and mass transit. He also founded the Eastern Montgomery County-Northeast Philadelphia Legislative Alliance, a group of local and state lawmakers who work across Northeast Philadelphia and Montgomery County on issues affecting both regions.\n\nBoyle was a founding member of the LGBT Equality Caucus during his first term in office, voting in favor of legislation in 2009 prohibiting discrimination of LGBT Pennsylvanians in work, housing and other areas the only time it passed out of committee.<ref>{{Cite web|title = House Committee Roll Call Votes - 2009 RCS# 88 |url=http://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/legis/RCC/PUBLIC/listVoteSummaryH.cfm?sYear=2009&sInd=0&cteeCde=36&theDate=03/11/2009&rNbr=88|website = The official website for the Pennsylvania General Assembly.|accessdate = 2015-12-05}}</ref> In 2014, he introduced legislation to amend Pennsylvania's hate crimes statutes to include crimes perpetrated based on sexual orientation.<!-- introduced but did it pass? --><ref name=\"biodata\" />\n\nBoyle also introduced legislation in 2011 to make [[genocide education]] a required part of Pennsylvania public school curricula, legislation that was eventually passed into law in 2014. In 2013, he introduced legislation to expand access to school counseling services, which resulted in him being selected as recipient of the 2013 Pennsylvania School Counselor Association's \"Legislator of the Year\" award. In 2014, he introduced the SAFER PA Act, which required timely testing of DNA evidence kits and that backlogged and untested evidence be reported to the state. It would also require that authorities notify victims or surviving family when DNA testing is completed. The SAFER PA Act was reintroduced and signed into law by Governor [[Tom Wolf (politician)|Tom Wolf]] in 2015<ref>{{Cite web|title = Bill Information - House Bill 2396; Regular Session 2011-2012|url = http://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/billinfo/billinfo.cfm?syear=2011&sind=0&body=H&type=B&bn=2396 |accessdate = 2015-12-05}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title = Bill Information - House Bill 1844; Regular Session 2013-2014 |url=http://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/billinfo/billinfo.cfm?syear=2013&sind=0&body=H&type=B&bn=1844|website = The official website for the Pennsylvania General Assembly.|accessdate = 2015-12-05}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title = House passes Boyle evidence registry bill {{!}} Broad Street Media|url = http://www.bsmphilly.com/2014/oct/22/house-passes-boyle-evidence-registry-bill/#.VmMO3_mrSM8|website = www.bsmphilly.com|accessdate = 2015-12-05}}</ref>\n\n===Committee assignments===\n*'''Appropriations'''\n*'''Insurance'''\n*'''Labor Relations'''\n*'''Liquor Control'''\n*'''Policy'''\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\n;2014\n{{See also|United States House of Representatives elections in Pennsylvania, 2014#District 13}}\nIn April 2013, Boyle announced his candidacy for [[Pennsylvania's 13th congressional district]], with the heavily Democratic leaning seat being vacated by the incumbent congressperson, [[Allyson Schwartz]], who declined to run for reelection to make an ultimately unsuccessful run for Governor. Boyle had the support of nearly 30 labor unions across the Philadelphia region.<ref name=\"Shaheeli\">{{cite web|author=Joe Shaheeli|title=Pols on the Street: Brendan Boyle Says He's In!|publisher=The Philadelphia Public Record|date=May 30, 2013 |url=http://www.phillyrecord.com/2013/05/pols-on-the-street-brendan-boyle-says-hes-in|accessdate=November 9, 2014}}</ref>[[File:Brendan Boyle.jpg|thumb|177x177px|Rep. He went on to win the seat in the general election on November 4, 2014, defeating Republican Carson \"Dee\" Adcock with 67% of the vote.<ref>[http://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/legis/home/member_information/house_bio.cfm?id=1160 Representative Brendan Boyle profile] Pennsylvania House of Representatives official website; accessed November 9, 2014.</ref><ref>{{CongLinks|votesmart=47357|fec=H4PA13199}}</ref>\n\n;2016\nNo Republican or other party candidate filed to run against Boyle in [[United States House of Representatives elections in Pennsylvania, 2016|2016]], so he was re-elected unopposed.\n\n;2018\nThe [[Supreme Court of Pennsylvania]] imposed a new map for [[Pennsylvania's congressional districts]] in February 2018.<ref>{{cite news |last1=Cohn |first1=Nate |last2=Bloch |first2=Matthew |last3=Quealy |first3=Kevin |date=February 19, 2018 |title=The New Pennsylvania Congressional Map, District by District |url=https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/02/19/upshot/pennsylvania-new-house-districts-gerrymandering.html |work=[[The New York Times]] |location=New York |access-date=February 22, 2018 }}</ref> Boyle then announced that he will run for re-election in the new [[Pennsylvania's 2nd congressional district|2nd District]].<ref>{{cite news |last=Kopp |first=John |date=February 22, 2018 |title=Brendan Boyle to seek re-election in redrawn Philly congressional district  |url=http://www.phillyvoice.com/brendan-boyle-seek-re-election-redrawn-philly-congressional-district/ |work=Philly Voice |location=Philadelphia, PA |access-date=February 22, 2018 }}</ref> That district had previously been the [[Pennsylvania's 1st congressional district|1st District]], represented by retiring fellow Democrat [[Bob Brady]]. However, the new 2nd absorbed all of the Philadelphia portion of the old 13th, including Boyle's home.<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/02/19/upshot/pennsylvania-new-house-districts-gerrymandering.html |newspaper=The New York Times |department=The Upshot |title=The New Pennsylvania House Districts Are In. He has supported legislation to raise the federal minimum wage to $12.00 an hour, as well as measures to revise the way Social Security benefits are calculated to protect seniors from seeing their benefits reduced over time.\n\nBoyle has been outspoken about the need to protect American jobs. After [[Mondelez International]] announced that it would close a Philadelphia factory, Boyle announced his support for the [[Oreo Boycott]] by appearing with a poster featuring an Oreo cookie red circle and line through it, accompanied by the message, \"Say no to Oreo,\" <ref name=northeast>{{cite news |url=http://www.northeasttimes.com/2015/jul/15/boyle-calls-nabisco-boycott/|title=Boyle calls for Nabisco boycott|author=Northeast Times Staff |date= July 15, 2015|accessdate=July 15, 2015 |publisher=Northeast Times}}</ref><ref name=inquirer/> After highlighting the American layoffs, Boyle noted that CEO Rosenfeld received a pay increase.<ref name=northeast/><ref name=inquirer>{{cite news|url=http://www.philly.com/philly/blogs/inq-phillydeals/Oreo-maker-faces-billionaire-squeeze-labor-boycott-calls.html|title=Oreo sees support, but also backlash and boycott, for gay pride rainbow cookie |author=Joseph N. DiStefano |date= August 12, 2015|accessdate=July 9, 2015|publisher=Philly.com}}</ref>\n\nBoyle filed the Standardizing Testing and Accountability Before Large Elections Giving Electors Necessary Information for Unobstructed Selection Act, or shortened to the acronym [[Stable Genius Act|Stable Genius]] Act, in 2018.
