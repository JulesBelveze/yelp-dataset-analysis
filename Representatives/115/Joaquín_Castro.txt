The district includes just over half of his native [[San Antonio]], Texas, as well as some of its nearby suburbs. From 2003 to 2013, Castro was a member of the [[Texas House of Representatives]] for District 125.<ref name=\"txgov\">[http://www.house.state.tx.us/members/dist125/castro.htm Texas House of Representatives membership summary] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20101018061426/http://www.house.state.tx.us/members/dist125/castro.htm |date=2010-10-18 }}</ref> While in the Texas state legislature, Castro served as vice-chair of the Higher Education Committee and was a member of the Judiciary & Civil Jurisprudence Committee. He also previously served on other committees, such as County Affairs, Border & International Affairs, and Juvenile Justice & Family Issues.<ref name=\"txgov\" />\n\nHis identical twin brother, [[Julian Castro|Juli\u00e1n Castro]], was the [[Mayor of San Antonio]] from 2009 to 2014 and [[Secretary of Housing and Urban Development]] under [[Barack Obama|President Barack Obama]] from 2014 to 2017.<ref name=\"SecHousing\">{{cite web |url=http://trailblazersblog.dallasnews.com/2014/07/julian-castro-to-take-office-monday-as-housing-secretary.html/ | title=Juli\u00e1n Castro to take office Monday as Housing Secretary | publisher=[[Dallas Morning News]] | date=25 July 2014 | author=Gillman, Todd J}}</ref>\n\n==Early life, education, and early career==\nCastro was born and raised in San Antonio and attended [[Thomas Jefferson High School (San Antonio, TX)|Thomas Jefferson High School]]. Castro has stated that his interest in public service developed at a young age from watching his parents' involvement in political campaigns and civic causes. His father, Jessie Guzman, is a retired [[mathematics]] teacher from the [[Edgewood Independent School District (Bexar County, Texas)|Edgewood Independent School District]] in the west side of San Antonio, and his mother, Marie \"Rosie\" Castro, a community activist. Jessie and Rosie never married. He graduated with honors from [[Stanford University]] with a [[Bachelor of Arts]] in [[political science]] and [[communications]] and earned a [[Juris Doctor]] with his twin brother at [[Harvard Law School]] in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts|Cambridge]], [[Massachusetts]].<ref name=\"house.state.tx.us\">{{cite web|url=http://www.house.state.tx.us/members/member-page/?district=125#member-biography|title=Texas House of Representatives|first=Texas House of|last=Representatives|website=www.house.state.tx.us}}</ref> After law school, the two brothers continued together to work for the law firm [[Akin Gump Strauss Hauer & Feld]] before starting their own firm in 2005.<ref>{{cite web|title=TRIBPEDIA: Juli\u00e1n Castro|publisher=\"[[The Texas Tribune]]\"|accessdate=2013-08-29 |url=http://www.texastribune.org/tribpedia/julian-castro/about/}}</ref>\n\nHe worked in [[public education]], [[health care]], and the juvenile justice system.<ref name=\"house.state.tx.us\"/> Castro is a member of the National College Advising Corps, [[St. Mary's University, Texas|St. Mary's University]] Mission and Identity Taskforce, [[St. Philip's College (United States)|St. Philip's College]] President's Advisory Board, and Texas Family Impact Seminar.\n\n==Texas House of Representatives==\n\n===Elections===\nCastro ran for Texas's 125th House district in 2002. He defeated incumbent State Representative [[Arthur Reyna]] in the Democratic primary 64-36 percent.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=290782|title=Our Campaigns - TX State House 125- D Primary Race - Mar 12, 2002|website=www.ourcampaigns.com}}</ref> In the general election, he defeated [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] Nelson Balido, 60-40 percent. He was twenty-eight at the time of his election to the state House.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=5909|title=Our Campaigns - TX State House 125 Race - Nov 05, 2002|website=www.ourcampaigns.com}}</ref> In 2004, he won re-election to a second term unopposed.<ref name=\"elections.sos.state.tx.us\">{{cite web|url=http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist.exe |title=Archived copy |accessdate=December 26, 2009 |deadurl=yes |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20140109062336/http://elections.sos.state.tx.us/elchist.exe |archivedate=January 9, 2014 }}</ref> In 2006, he won re-election to a third term, defeating Republican Nelson Balido, 58%-38%.<ref name=\"elections.sos.state.tx.us\"/> In 2008, he won re-election to a fourth term unopposed.<ref name=\"elections.sos.state.tx.us\"/> In 2010, he won re-election to a fifth term, defeating Libertarian Jeffrey Blunt, 78%-22%.<ref name=\"elections.sos.state.tx.us\"/>\n\n===Committee assignments===\n*County Affairs\n*Higher Education (Vice Chair)<ref name=\"Garrett\"/>\n*Judiciary & Civil Jurisprudence\n*Oversight of Higher Ed Governance, Excellence & Transparency<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.legis.state.tx.us/Members/MemberInfo.aspx?Leg=82&Chamber=H&Code=A2495|title=Texas Legislature Online - 82nd Legislature - Information for Rep. Joaquin Castro|website=www.legis.state.tx.us}}</ref>\n\n==U.S. House of Representatives==\n===Elections===\nIn June 2011, Castro announced that he was running for a seat in the [[United States House of Representatives]] in the newly-drawn {{ushr|TX|35}}.  He was initially set to challenge fellow Democrat and nine-term incumbent [[Lloyd Doggett]], whose home in [[Austin, Texas|Austin]] had been drawn into the district, in the Democratic primary<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.texastribune.org/texas-politics/2012-congressional-election/castro-to-take-on-doggett-for-congressional-seat|title=Castro To Take On Doggett for New Congressional Seat \u2014 2012 Congressional Election |last=Ramshaw|first=Emily|date=June 24, 2011|work=Texas Tribune|accessdate=5 September 2012}}</ref>\n\nHowever, on November 28, after [[Charlie Gonzalez]] of the neighboring [[Texas's 20th congressional district|20th District]] announced his retirement after seven terms, Castro announced his intent to run instead for the 20th District seat. He was unopposed in the Democratic primary, all but assuring him of being the next congressman from this heavily Democratic, Hispanic-majority district. At the [[2012 Democratic National Convention]], he introduced his brother Juli\u00e1n as keynote speaker.<ref name = \"Garrett\">{{cite news|url=http://www.dallasnews.com/news/politics/national-politics/20120904-with-his-twin-brother-in-the-spotlight-joaquin-castro-prepares-for-prominent-role-of-his-own.ece|title=With his twin brother in the spotlight, Joaquin Castro prepares for prominent role of his own|last=Garrett|first=Robert T.|date=September 4, 2012|work=Dallas Morning News|accessdate=5 September 2012}}</ref> In November 2012, Castro defeated Republican David Rosa 64%-34%.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ourcampaigns.com/RaceDetail.html?RaceID=750688|title=Our Campaigns - TX District 20 Race - Nov 06, 2012|website=www.ourcampaigns.com}}</ref> becoming only the fifth person to represent this district since its creation in 1935.\n\nIn 2017, a columnist for the ''[[San Antonio Express-News]]'' questioned Castro's decision not to enter the [[United States Senate election in Texas, 2018|2018 U.S. Senate race]] against the Republican incumbent [[Ted Cruz]], an unsuccessful presidential candidate in [[U.S. presidential election, 2016|2016]]. Bruce Davidson predicted that Castro could have defeated the announced candidate, [[Beto O'Rourke]], representative of [[Texas's 16th congressional district]] based in [[El Paso, Texas|El Paso]], for the Democratic senatorial nomination. \"Castro is said to be ambitious, but will he ever have a better chance to move up than in the Trump-era against Ted Cruz?,\" Davidson asked. Davidson added that Texas' other senator, Republican [[John Cornyn]] of San Antonio, would have taken advantage of a similar opportunity to run. In 2002, Cornyn, the state's then one-term [[Attorney General of Texas|attorney general]], filed to succeed retiring Republican Senator [[Phil Gramm]], while two other Republican hopefuls, [[Henry Bonilla]] of [[Texas's 23rd congressional district]] and [[David Dewhurst]], the [[Texas General Land Office|land commissioner]] and later the [[Lieutenant Governor of Texas|lieutenant governor]], vacillated and lost their chance to become a senator. Bonilla was defeated for House re-election after redistricting in 2006, and Dewhurst subsequently lost the 2012 Republican runoff election for the Senate to Ted Cruz.<ref>Bruce Davidson, \"Risk-averse Castro opts out of Senate run\", ''[[San Antonio Express-News]],'' May 14, 2017, F3.</ref>\n\n===Tenure===\n[[Image:Joaquin Castro Keynote.jpg|thumb|right|Representative Castro preparing to deliver a keynote speech at [[League of United Latin American Citizens|LULAC]].]]\n\nCastro was officially sworn into office on January 3, 2013 becoming a member of the [[113th United States Congress]].
