A [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]], Yoder was the [[Kansas House of Representatives|Kansas State Representative]] for the 20th district from 2003 to 2011.\n\n==Early life and education==\nYoder was born and raised on a grain and livestock farm in [[Yoder, Kansas]], a small farming town outside of [[Hutchinson, Kansas|Hutchinson]]. He is the son of Susan Elizabeth Peck (n\u00e9e Alexander) and Wayne E. Yoder. His ancestry includes Northern Irish, German, and English.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://freepages.genealogy.rootsweb.ancestry.com/%7Ebattle/reps/yoder.htm |title=Kevin Yoder ancestry |website=Ancestry.com |accessdate= August 13, 2014}}</ref>\n\nYoder graduated from Hutchinson High School and, in 1999, from the [[University of Kansas]] with a dual major in English and [[Political Science]]. He served as KU Student Body president, president of the Kansas Union Memorial Corporation Board of Directors, and as a board member of the KU Athletics Corporation. He was a member of [[Lambda Chi Alpha]] fraternity, served as president, and received the 2012 Order of Achievement award from Lambda Chi Alpha.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.lambdachi.org/candc/congressmen-and-passionate-brothers |title=Congressmen and Passionate Brothers |publisher= Lambda Chi Alpha |date=January 5, 2011 |accessdate= August 13, 2014}}</ref> While at KU, Yoder interned with the Kansas State Legislature. In 2002, he received a law degree from the [[University of Kansas Law School]] where he served for two years as Student Bar Association President.<ref name =\"yoderweb\">[http://www.yoderforcongress.com/about About Kevin], Kevin for Congress website</ref> Yoder has served on the KU Law School Board of Governors. He was a 2007 graduate of Leadership Kansas.\n\n==Law career==\nYoder worked as a law clerk for Payne and Jones from 2000 to 2001, then as a special assistant in the [[U.S. Department of Defense]]'s Office of Counternarcotics in 2001.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://votesmart.org/bio.php?can_id=34433 |title=Representative Kevin W. Yoder (KS) |publisher=[[Project Vote Smart]]}}</ref> He joined Speer and Holliday LLP, a small law firm in Olathe, as an associate and became a partner in 2005.\n\nYoder is a member of the American Council of Young Political Leaders and the [[Kansas Bar Association]], and has served on the Board of Directors of the Johnson County Bar Association.\n\n==Kansas House of Representatives==\n\nYoder was first elected to the Kansas House of Representatives (20th district) in 2003. He was then subsequently re-elected to the office three times.<ref name=\"yoderweb\"/> The district includes portions of [[Overland Park]] and [[Leawood]].\n\nAs chair of the Kansas State [[House Appropriations Committee]], he had the responsibility to balance the budget, cut government spending, oppose raising taxes, and allocate over $13 billion in state revenue to public schools, universities, prisons, social services and highways. In 2016, Yoder tried to convince \"the most ardent or strident conservatives in the [[United States House of Representatives|House of Representatives]] to get them to embrace research\" as a fiscally and morally responsible thing to fund. More than 100 House [[Republican Party (United States)|Republicans]], including conservative members like [[Dave Brat]], signed onto his letter to House leadership pushing for a $3 billion bump.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2016/01/national-institutes-of-health-congress-budget/423837/|title=What's Next for the National Institutes of Health?|last=Kelly|first=Nora|language=en-US|access-date=2016-07-18}}</ref> In the end, Congress provided the largest funding increase for research in 12 years.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.statnews.com/2015/12/16/congress-nih-funding-increase/|title=Congress gives big funding increase to NIH|date=2015-12-16|website=STAT|access-date=2016-07-18}}</ref> At the end of that year, he penned a guest column for [[Fox News]], arguing that support for cures to diseases and federal funding for medical research could be an issue to rally a divided country following the [[United States presidential election, 2016|2016 presidential election]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.foxnews.com/opinion/2016/11/30/rep-kevin-yoder-heres-cause-to-unite-trump-and-clinton-voters.html|title=Rep. Yoder broke ranks, siding with 190 Democrats (and 14 Republicans) when he voted against allowing internet providers to snoop on users and sell their personal online history.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.kansascity.com/opinion/editorials/article142045569.html|title=Editorial: Rep. [http://thehill.com/policy/technology/209730-house-email-privacy-bill-hits-magic-number \"Bill requiring warrants for email searches hits magic number in House\"], The Hill.</ref> Congress passed it in 2016 by a vote of 419-0, and again by a unanimous vote in 2017.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://thehill.com/policy/technology/277897-house-unanimously-passes-bill-to-protect-email-privacy|title=House unanimously passes email privacy bill|last=Trujillo|first=Mario|date=2016-04-27|access-date=2016-07-18}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://fcw.com/articles/2017/02/07/ecpa-passes-house-again.aspx|title=House passes email privacy act, again -- FCW|website=FCW|language=en|access-date=2018-01-10}}</ref>\n\nYoder also reintroduced the [[Murder of Kelsey Smith#Kelsey Smith Act|Kelsey Smith Act]], legislation that required cell phone carriers to provide location information to the authorities in situations involving \"risk of death or serious physical injury.\"<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://yoder.house.gov/media-center/press-releases/rep-yoder-reintroduces-kelsey-smith-act-to-help-prevent-violent-crimes|title=Rep. Yoder Reintroduces Kelsey Smith Act to Help Prevent Violent Crimes|date=2016-03-23|access-date=2016-07-18}} {{PD-notice}}</ref> In May 2016, the bill failed to receive the two-thirds required majority of the [[United States House of Representatives|House of Representatives]] to pass under a procedural hurdle, due to privacy concerns.<ref>{{Cite
